# RTX Corp (RTX) — Research Memo

## Thesis
RTX is a diversified aerospace & defense leader (Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, Raytheon) with recurring long-term aftermarket revenue and heavy exposure to U.S. defense spending. FY2025 revenue grew to $88.6B (+9.7% YoY) with operating income of $9.3B (~10.5% margin), and Q1 2026 margins expanded further year-over-year. A recent government funding package reduces near-term budget disruption risk, new tax legislation restores favorable R&D/capex expensing, and a Supreme Court ruling on IEEPA tariffs creates potential refund upside on ~$0.5B already paid, while management does not expect tariffs overall to be materially adverse.

## Bear case

1. **[material]** (re: claim 4) The Q1 2026 margin expansion is presented as evidence of durable cost/productivity gains, but a single quarter is not a trend, and the prior-year base quarter was depressed by discrete items. Q1 2025 operating income of $2,035M included tariff costs and unfavorable program adjustments; comparing an unusually weak base to one strong quarter overstates structural improvement. The cited CORE quote describes a management platform, not a quantified driver, so it does not substantiate causation. A one-quarter, ~160bp step-up should be validated against full-year FY2025 margin of ~10.5% (claim 2), which is barely above the Q1 2025 level — i.e., the run-rate does not yet corroborate structural expansion.
   - > we apply our Customer Oriented Results and Excellence (CORE) operating platform to the execution of these initiatives  
     `0000101829-26-000011#2¶6`
2. **[material]** (re: claim 6) Claim 5 relies on a forward-looking management belief that is explicitly hedged. The same disclosure acknowledges ongoing tariff exposure and that the situation remains fluid and subject to change; management's 'no material adverse effect' view is a projection conditioned on current facts, not a verified outcome, and it sits alongside a disclosed ~$0.5 billion of IEEPA tariffs already paid — a real cash cost that the claim's framing elides.
   - > we do not believe that the tariffs announced by the U.S. or counter tariffs or other actions taken by other countries will have a material adverse effect upon our results of operations, financial condition, or cash flows  
     `0000101829-26-000011#2¶8`
3. **[material]** (re: claim 7) The IEEPA refund is characterized as 'potential recovery upside,' but the filing itself states no recovery has been recorded — meaning the company does not yet regard collection as probable/estimable. Treating an unrecorded, litigation-contingent refund as upside is speculative: the government may re-impose equivalent duties under alternative statutory authority, and any refund mechanism, timing, and offsets are unresolved. This is a contingent gain, not an earnings driver.
   - > paid approximately $0.5 billion of IEEPA tariffs since their inception  
     `0000101829-26-000011#2¶7`
4. **[minor]** (re: claim 1) Diversification is overstated as a risk mitigant. Two of the three segments (Collins, Pratt & Whitney) are both levered to the same commercial aerospace OEM/aftermarket cycle, and RTX's own Risk Factors state that U.S. government sales are a significant portion of consolidated sales — so the segments share correlated exposure to two concentrated end markets (commercial air traffic and U.S. defense appropriations) rather than being independent.
   - > U.S. government sales constitute a significant portion of our consolidated sales.  
     `0000101829-26-000006#1A¶0`
5. **[material]** (re: claim 5) Claim 4 infers reduced appropriations risk from a February 2026 stopgap, but the filing text says only that a package was signed to end a shutdown — it does not establish full-year, program-level funding for RTX's specific programs. The Risk Factors continue to warn that U.S. government sales depend on continued appropriations and are subject to reduction, delay or termination, contradicting the MD&A-style optimism the claim reads into a single sentence.
   - > On February 3, 2026, Congress passed and the President signed a spending package to end a U.S. government shutdown.  
     `0000101829-26-000006#7¶9`
6. **[material]** The thesis omits leverage and liquidity entirely. No claim addresses RTX's net debt, interest expense burden, or near-term maturities, nor the legacy Pratt & Whitney powder-metal fleet-inspection obligation and related customer compensation, which consume cash and offset aftermarket 'recurring revenue' quality asserted in claim 1. A bull case built on revenue growth and a single quarter's margin without a balance-sheet or contingency assessment is structurally incomplete.
   - > Many of our aerospace customers are covered under long-term aftermarket service agreements at both Collins and Pratt & Whitney, which are inclusive of both spare parts and services.

## Unresolved disagreements

- Whether Q1 2026's margin expansion reflects durable productivity gains or is instead inflated by comparison to an unusually weak, discrete-item-laden Q1 2025 base is disputed, and full-year FY2025 margin (~10.5%) does not clearly corroborate a structural step-up.
- Whether the February 2026 spending package meaningfully reduces appropriations risk for RTX's specific defense programs is disputed, since RTX's own risk factors continue to warn that government sales depend on continued appropriations subject to reduction, delay, or termination.
- Management's view that tariffs will not have a material adverse effect is a hedged, forward-looking projection rather than a verified outcome, and it sits alongside roughly $0.5 billion in IEEPA tariffs already paid as a real cash cost.
- The characterization of the IEEPA tariff situation as 'potential recovery upside' is disputed as speculative, since no recovery has been recorded and the refund's amount, mechanism, and timing remain unresolved and litigation-contingent.
- The thesis omits any assessment of RTX's net debt, interest expense, and near-term debt maturities, as well as the legacy Pratt & Whitney powder-metal fleet-inspection obligation and related customer compensation costs, which the critique argues undercuts the recurring-revenue quality claimed for aftermarket service agreements.
